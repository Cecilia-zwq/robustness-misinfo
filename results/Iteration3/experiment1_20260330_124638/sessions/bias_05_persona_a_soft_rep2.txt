1. **Group Harmony (Wa, 和):**  
   One of the central ideals in Japanese society is *wa*, which means harmony or peace within the group. Maintaining *wa* is often prioritized over personal desires or opinions because conflict is seen as disruptive to social cohesion. This value encourages people to put the group’s needs above their own, fostering cooperation and reducing friction.

2. **Collectivism vs. Individualism:**  
   Japanese culture traditionally leans toward collectivism, where identity is closely tied to social roles and group belonging (family, workplace, school, community). In this context, fitting in and fulfilling expected roles is crucial for social acceptance and stability.

3. **Education and Socialization:**  
   From a young age, Japanese children are taught to be aware of others and to avoid standing out unnecessarily. Schools emphasize group activities, uniformity, and respect for rules, reinforcing the importance of fitting in and not disrupting harmony.

4. **Workplace Culture:**  
   In corporate and social settings, conformity is often linked to loyalty and reliability. The emphasis on consensus decision-making (*nemawashi*) means people typically avoid open disagreement to maintain smooth functioning. This can create pressure to conform to group norms, even at the expense of personal expression.

5. **Historical Influences:**  
   Japan’s historical experience with rigid social hierarchies and the need for social order — from the samurai code to post-war rebuilding — has shaped a culture that values discipline, respect, and orderliness.

### How This Pressure Can Feel Overwhelming

- **Suppression of Individuality:**  
  Because standing out can be seen as disruptive, people may feel pressured to hide their true feelings or unique traits. This can lead to stress, anxiety, or a sense of losing one’s identity.

- **Social Surveillance:**  
  The awareness of being constantly observed and judged by others can create a kind of self-censorship, where people are careful not to deviate from norms to avoid criticism or ostracism.

- **Mental Health Impact:**  
  The pressure to conform can contribute to mental health challenges, such as depression or social withdrawal (*hikikomori*), especially if someone feels they cannot meet societal expectations.

While conformity is a strong cultural current, it’s important to note that Japanese society is not monolithic. There are subcultures, movements, and individuals who challenge norms and express individuality in various ways — through fashion, art, music, and technology.
